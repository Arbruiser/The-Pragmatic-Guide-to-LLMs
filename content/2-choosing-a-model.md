# Choosing a model

You are likely already familiar with proprietary language models like OpenAI's GPT series or Anthropic's Claude. On LUMI, however, you have the computing power to run, modify, and fine-tune% **open-weight%** models — and choosing the right one is the first practical decision of any LLM project.

## Open-weight models

When you hear names like **Llama** (by Meta), **Mistral**, or **Qwen**, these are all highly capable open-weight alternatives to proprietary models. Because their underlying weights are publicly available — most commonly via [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/) — you are not restricted by API% limits, vendor lock-in%, or data privacy concerns. You can fine-tune and test them securely and privately on LUMI's hardware, and your data never leaves the system.

## Judging model quality

**Parameter count is a useful first filter** — a 70B model will generally outperform a 7B model from the same family — but it is far from the whole story. Recent models often *vastly* outperform older models of the same size thanks to better training data and improved architectures. So how do you compare models?

The most obvious answer is to use standardized tests, but this introduces **the benchmark problem**. There is a myriad of benchmarks (like SWE-Bench, Humanity's Last Exam, or MMLU) that test everything from agentic coding to multilingual reasoning. However, no single benchmark tells the whole story, and many are actively flawed:

- **Data contamination:** Because LLMs are trained on internet data, the answers to benchmark tests are often accidentally or intentionally included in their training data. The model may have "memorised" the test rather than learned to reason.
- **Over-optimisation:** When a benchmark becomes popular, model developers optimise specifically to score well on it, even if real-world quality suffers.
- **Subjective "vibes":** A model might score perfectly on a standardised test but feel stiff, overly formal, or unhelpful in actual interactions.

Ultimately, the only *true* test of model quality is running it against your specific domain use-case. Benchmarks help you build a shortlist; your own evaluation picks the winner.

## Base vs. Instruct models

When browsing repositories like Hugging Face, you will often see two versions of the same model: a **Base** model and an **Instruct** (or Chat) model. Both have the exact same goal — predict the most likely next token% — but they differ in what they were trained on:

- **Base models** undergo a massive pre-training phase where they "read" vast amounts of raw text from the internet. However, their only behaviour is to continue a pattern from the input. If you prompt a Base model with *"The capital of France is"*, it will correctly answer *"Paris"*. But if you prompt it with *"What is the capital of France?"*, it might just continue the pattern of questions and output *"What is the capital of Germany? What is the capital of Ukraine?"*. Base models are raw pattern-matchers. While they are the foundational starting point for creating custom models, turning a raw Base model into a polished assistant requires a complex, data-heavy post-training pipeline that you must build yourself.
- **Instruct (Chat) models** are Base models that have undergone additional training — collectively called **post-training** — to turn a raw text predictor into a usable assistant. Post-training teaches the model to treat your input as an instruction and produce a helpful answer, usually in two (or more) stages:
  1. **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT):** The model is trained on a curated dataset of instruction–response pairs: prompts (*"Summarise this article"*, *"Write a Python function that..."*) each paired with a high-quality answer. The training objective is the same next-token prediction used in pretraining — the model simply learns to imitate the demonstrated answers. SFT teaches the model the basic format of being an assistant: answer the question, follow the instruction, use the chat structure, and stop when done. After SFT, the model is already usable as a chatbot, but its answers are only as good as its imitation of the training examples.
  2. **Preference Optimisation:** Imitation alone has limits — for many prompts there's no single "correct" answer, but some answers are clearly better than others (e.g. more accurate, safer, less verbose). So the next stage trains the model on comparisons. A widely used technique is **DPO** (Direct Preference Optimisation). The training data consists of triples: a prompt plus two candidate responses, one labelled as preferred. (These responses are typically generated by a model and then ranked by human annotators or by a strong LLM acting as a judge). DPO updates the model's weights to increase the probability of the preferred answer relative to the rejected one, while staying anchored to the SFT model so it doesn't drift too far. DPO is popular — especially for open-weight models — because it works entirely offline on a fixed dataset, with no need for the model to generate text or for a separate reward model during training.

> [!tip] Which one should you fine-tune?
> Most users should start their fine-tuning on an **Instruct** model. This gives you the basic chat format, instruction-following capabilities, and safety guardrails "for free," allowing you to focus your fine-tuning purely on injecting your domain knowledge (like legal terminology or specific coding frameworks). You should only fine-tune a Base model if you need absolute, total control over the post-training recipe — a process that is highly complex, requires deep technical expertise, and demands massive amounts of extremely high-quality data.

### Reasoning models

Most modern instruct models also come in a **reasoning** (or "thinking") variant. Instead of immediately blurting out an answer, these models are post-trained on datasets containing detailed internal monologues, teaching them to generate "reasoning tokens" to think through a problem step-by-step before providing the final response. 

To the model, these reasoning tokens are exactly the same as normal text tokens. The separation is created by a special boundary token (like `</think>`). Everything generated before this token is the model's internal reasoning, and everything after is the final answer meant for the user. This improves accuracy on complex tasks (like maths and coding) not simply because there are more tokens, but because it gives the model "space" to explore multiple viewpoints and work through the logic step-by-step before committing to a final answer — much like how humans think before they speak (at least, some do). The trade-off is that this extended internal monologue dramatically increases the compute cost per request.

> [!tip] The hidden thoughts of proprietary models
> If you use proprietary reasoning models like OpenAI's GPT or Google's Gemini, you might notice they do not expose their full, raw thought process to the user—only brief, sanitised summaries (An exception is Anthropic's Claude which explicitly exposes extended thinking). Hiding the internal monologue is a deliberate measure for several reasons. First, to prevent **distillation**: vendors do not want competitors scraping% their high-quality reasoning traces to use as free training data for their own models. Second, for **safety and UX**: hiding the raw thoughts allows the model to explore potentially unfiltered or complex ideas internally without exposing confusing or unaligned% text to the user, as long as the final output is safe.

## Key takeaways

- Open-weight models give you full control: no API limits, no vendor lock-in, and your data stays on LUMI.
- Benchmarks are a starting point, not a verdict — contamination and over-optimisation make them unreliable. Test candidates on your own use-case.
- Download the **Instruct** version unless you are fine-tuning from scratch.
